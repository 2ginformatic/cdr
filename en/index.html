Its germination was estimated at 2832 BC, so its age at 2019 is 4851 years.

The origin of life on Earth can be dated to a period between 4.4 billion years ago, when liquid water appeared on the earth's surface. It is believed, however, that life originated around 3.9 billion years, when the earth began to cool down to a temperature at which water could be widely found in the liquid state. The findings of microbial structures dating back 3.7 billion years ago in Greenland confirm this.

The human being

The human being belongs to the species classified as Homo Sapiens, which is part of the primate order and is the product of an evolution lasting millions of years. Human beings are running at a speed of eight billion - today we are at 7.6 billion - but they represent only 0.01% of the living beings on Earth.

Evidently the presence of the human is decidedly cumbersome, despite its extremely low relative weight, mainly due to its extreme efficiency in the use of natural resources.

Since the appearance of mankind, wild animals have reduced by 83% while the plants have halved. Above all the wild forms have been replaced by those bred: 70% of the birds on the planet are made up of poultry and only 30% are wild.

Charles Darwin's theory of evolution

All organisms, including man, originated over time from older life forms. This means that every living species descends from other species, therefore all living beings have a common ancestor in the distant past. According to Darwin, therefore, the environment has an indirect role in evolutionary processes in that it does not provoke the appearance of new characters, but makes the individuals who present characters that are particularly favorable in that environment survive better and reproduce themselves more than the other individuals with less suitable characters.

For Darwin, the ancestors of giraffes had to have necks of varying lengths. When the grass in the savannah started to run low, the long-necked giraffes were favored in reaching the leaves on the branches of the acacia trees. more suited to the variations imposed by the environment, surviving, compared to those with a short neck.

The impact of human on the environment

The impact of man on nature is universally recognized as alarming, such as to strongly threaten our quality of life. It is the first time, in the history of life on earth, that a single species is able to influence radically the fate of all others, animals and plants, upsetting and destroying ecosystems. Today's man is convinced he can live without depending on nature, but he is wrong: he underestimates his relationship with it, underestimates his dependence on the natural world and his profound vulnerability, exploiting it and trying to become its master.

Man, without doubt, is the number one enemy of biodiversity. An enemy that cannot therefore be eliminated. Rather, conservation requires a deep understanding of the complex links between the environment and human populations. Of course this is not a simple undertaking. Very often, to complicate the task of conservationists, there is a close link between the so-called hot spots, ie sites particularly rich in biodiversity, and poverty.

Desertification and deforestation

Like all species, man modifies the territory in which he finds himself to make it more comfortable and bring it closer to his survival needs. This activity conducted in areas adjacent to desert areas has enormously speeded up the natural process of desertification. As a result, arid but still capable of hosting life is gradually becoming uninhabitable. Intensive crops, combined with efforts to supply timber, have also triggered an increasingly rapid deforestation.

Increasingly larger portions of forests have been torn down with tragic consequences for biodiversity. The large rainforests of South America and Central Africa alone are home to about half of the living species on our planet.

Aggravated by the intertwining of various factors that multiply its effects, pollution also affects the areas most distant from modern civilization, because it expands through the air and water routes. Clean energy

Renewable energies are generated from energy sources that are regenerated at the same speed with which they are consumed. They are therefore alternative forms of energy to the traditional fossil sources and many of them have the particularity of being also clean energies, that is, of not introducing into the atmosphere harmful substances and / or that could alter the climate.

The so-called "classic" renewable sources have been exploited for the production of electricity since the beginning of the industrial age. They essentially include hydropower and geothermal energy.

The prospects for the future use of classical renewable sources depend on the exploration of the potential resources available, particularly in developing countries and from demands in relation to the environment and social acceptance.
